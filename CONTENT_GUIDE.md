# Evidence-Informed Planner Content Guide

Use this guide whenever a topic is converted into planner pages. A planner supports reflection and action; it does not diagnose, treat, promise outcomes, or replace professional care.

## One page, one job

Give every page a `concept` that can be stated as one useful verb phrase: notice energy, choose one priority, plan recovery, reframe a setback, ask for support. If two pages have the same answer to “what changes after completing this?”, merge or redesign one. Each page should move through a distinct micro-arc: arrive, notice, choose, act, close.

## Prompt design

- Ask one thing at a time. Use plain, specific language and optional framing: “If it helps…”, “Choose one…”, “A few words are enough.”
- Prefer observable and answerable questions over broad demands. “What made this task easier to begin?” is better than “Why am I unmotivated?”
- Pair reflection with a small next action. Use if-then planning where appropriate: “If ___ happens, then I will ___.”
- Offer bounded choices before blank writing space when cognitive load may be high.
- Reinforce agency without forced positivity. Validate difficulty, notice effort, and invite realistic support.
- Include permission to stop, skip, or seek help on sensitive pages. Never force trauma recounting.
- Avoid shame, absolutes, toxic positivity, moral judgments, diagnostic claims, and promises that a worksheet will improve a condition.
- Do not ask for medication changes or clinical decisions. For crisis-adjacent topics, include a clearly separated note directing users to local emergency/crisis support and trusted people.

## Page variety matrix

Across a set, rotate both the thinking task and response form. Do not repeat the same combination on adjacent pages.

Thinking tasks: notice, name, sort, choose, sequence, anticipate, rehearse, connect, appreciate, review.

Response forms: scale, checkboxes, short phrases, spacious journaling, priority ranking, if-then plan, timeline, two-column comparison, support map, tracker, decision table, guided steps.

A set should not use the same section title or prompt twice. Repeated trackers are allowed only when repetition is the functional concept; their reflection prompts must still vary.

## Recommended page arc

1. Arrival: a low-effort check-in or context cue.
2. Focus: the page’s single concept, visually dominant.
3. Translation: turn insight into one realistic choice or next step.
4. Encouraging close: a brief, non-prescriptive prompt that notices effort, support, or what can be released.

## Content review checklist

- The page has exactly one primary concept.
- Every prompt contributes to that concept.
- The likely answer is useful, safe, and within the user’s control.
- The page works on a hard day, not only an ideal day.
- The response format matches the mental effort requested.
- Wording is distinct from every other page.
- The page ends with agency, support, or a feasible next step.
- Sensitive claims are reviewed by a qualified professional before publication.

## Evidence basis

These principles draw on humane, practical, non-intrusive support; listening without forcing disclosure; connecting people with needs and social support; structured self-help; and implementation-intention research. Maintain an evidence date and recheck sources for health-related products before publication.
